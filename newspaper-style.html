Millennium Park Redefines Urban Public Spaces Across America

Chicago's Crown Jewel Continues to Inspire City Planners and Draw Millions of Visitors Annually

By SARAH MITCHELL | Photography by JOHN ANDERSON March 26, 2026
C

HICAGO — On a crisp spring morning, as the first rays of sunlight glint off the polished surface of Anish Kapoor's "Cloud Gate" sculpture, dozens of early visitors gather in Millennium Park, cameras in hand, waiting for that perfect shot. The 110-ton stainless steel marvel, affectionately known as "The Bean," has become more than just public art—it's a symbol of Chicago's commitment to accessible culture and innovative urban design.

Since its opening in 2004, Millennium Park has transformed 24.5 acres of downtown Chicago into one of the world's most celebrated urban spaces. The park welcomes more than 25 million visitors annually, making it one of the most visited attractions in the Midwest.

"It's not just a park. It's a statement about what cities can be."
— Dr. Maria Rodriguez, Urban Planning Expert

The park's success lies in its diverse offerings. Beyond Cloud Gate, visitors discover the Crown Fountain, a revolutionary installation by Spanish artist Jaume Plensa that projects video images of Chicago residents' faces onto 50-foot glass towers. During summer months, children splash in the reflecting pool while adults appreciate the artistic and technological innovation.

Deep Dish Pizza Wars Heat Up in Chicago

By MICHAEL CHEN

The battle for the city's best deep dish pizza continues as local institutions and newcomers vie for recognition. Lou Malnati's, Giordano's, and Pequod's maintain their positions as top contenders, each offering distinct takes on Chicago's signature dish.

"It's not just about the crust or the cheese," says Tony Malnati, third-generation owner. "It's about maintaining tradition while innovating for new generations."

Chicago Hot Dog Stands Preserve Culinary Heritage

By JENNIFER TAYLOR

Portillo's and other classic hot dog stands continue to serve Vienna Beef franks "dragged through the garden"—topped with mustard, onions, relish, tomato, pickle, sport peppers, and celery salt. But never, ever ketchup.

The Chicago-style hot dog represents more than fast food; it's a culinary tradition dating back over a century, reflecting the city's immigrant heritage and working-class roots.
